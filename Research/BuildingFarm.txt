1 Pre‑Planning: Site Selection, Regulations and Taxes
1.1 Zoning and Right‑to‑Farm Protections
Local zoning determines where farming is permitted. Michigan’s Zoning Enabling Act allows townships and counties to divide land into districts and restrict uses[1]. Agricultural activity is generally allowed in rural districts but may require a special permit in suburban jurisdictions[1]. Farmers should contact planning officials to verify that proposed uses align with the zoning designation. The Michigan Right to Farm Act (RTFA) protects farms that meet the definition of a commercial farm and follow Generally Accepted Agricultural and Management Practices (GAAMPs) from nuisance suits and conflicting ordinances[1]. GAAMPs cover manure management, site selection for livestock facilities, animal care, nutrient utilization, irrigation water use and pesticide utilization[1]. Farms that comply with GAAMPs enjoy immunity from many local restrictions[1].
Per‑acre cost: Zoning and RTFA compliance involve administrative effort but no direct per‑acre cost. Because GAAMPs are freely available and compliance prevents future liability, the implied cost is minimal (<$1 per acre) and primarily involves time spent consulting regulators.
1.2 Building Codes and Permits
Michigan’s State Construction Code exempts structures that are “incidental to the use for agricultural purposes of the land on which the building is located” from its definition of a building[2]. Pole barns, freestall barns, hoop structures and other farm buildings used exclusively for production generally do not require state building permits. Local governments may still request an agricultural building exemption form and may regulate electrical and plumbing installations. Livestock facilities with large manure storage or high animal numbers may require permits under the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) or the Michigan Department of Environment, Great Lakes and Energy. Farmers should check whether irrigation wells exceeding 70 gallons per minute need registration and whether wetlands or waterways require permits. Permit fees vary, but the administrative cost is small relative to other expenses.
Per‑acre cost: Filing for agricultural building exemptions and state environmental permits typically costs $200–$500 per facility. Spreading this over a 100‑acre farm equals roughly $2–$5 per acre.
1.3 Property Taxes and Farmland Preservation
Michigan taxes farmland at fair market value instead of agricultural use value. A legislative analysis of farmland preservation programs noted that Michigan farmers pay $17–$20 per acre in property taxes compared with $7–$10 nationally[3]. Farmers can enroll in farmland preservation agreements (Public Act 116) to receive income‑tax credits for keeping land in agriculture[3]. Conservation easements through land trusts may also reduce taxes and prevent non‑agricultural development.
Per‑acre cost: Property taxes average $18.50 per acre for Michigan farms[3]. Participation in preservation programs can offset this cost through tax credits but does not eliminate it.
2 Land Acquisition and Financing
2.1 Farmland Values and Rental Rates
USDA’s National Agricultural Statistics Service reported that Michigan farm real estate (land and buildings) averaged $6,310 per acre in 2024[4]. Cropland averaged $5,870 per acre and pasture averaged $2,970 per acre[4]. Cash rent for cropland averaged $152 per acre[4]. These figures provide baseline purchase or lease values.
Per‑acre cost: Purchasing cropland costs $6,310 per acre[4] while leasing costs roughly $152 per acre per year. Pasture costs $2,970 per acre[4].
2.2 Financing and Transaction Costs
Buying farmland usually requires a down payment of 20–30 %. Closing costs include legal fees, title insurance and appraisal services, often totaling $300–$1,000 per transaction. Interest rates for farm mortgages vary with market conditions; many producers use Farm Service Agency loans with reduced down payments. For budgeting, allocate 25 % of the purchase price as cash (for down payment and closing) and finance the remainder. Spreading closing costs across a 100‑acre purchase adds approximately $3–$10 per acre.
Per‑acre cost: Assuming a $6,310 purchase price per acre, a 25 % down payment adds $1,577 per acre to the initial outlay. Transaction fees add $3–$10 per acre.
3 Soil Evaluation and Land Preparation
3.1 Soil Testing and Mapping
Soil tests reveal nutrient levels, pH and organic matter. Michigan State University (MSU) Extension sells soil‑test bags for $18 per sample[5] and recommends one sample per 5–10 acres. A 100‑acre farm therefore requires roughly ten samples, costing $180. Surveyors can prepare a topographic map and legal description for $500–$1,500 per farm; dividing by 100 acres yields $5–$15 per acre. Farmland with wetlands or endangered species may need environmental assessments; costs vary but typically add $500–$2,000.
Per‑acre cost: Soil testing and mapping cost around $2.0–$3.0 per acre (testing) plus $5–$15 per acre for surveying, totalling $7–$18 per acre.
3.2 Site Clearing, Grading and Lane Construction
Cornell Cooperative Extension’s building cost guide estimates that general site preparation costs $5 per square foot[6] and that stripping topsoil costs $3–$3.50 per cubic yard[6]. Clearing vegetation and grading are only necessary for areas hosting buildings, lanes and paddocks. Clearing 10 % of a 100‑acre farm (435,600 ft²) at $5/ft² costs $2.18 million, or $21,780 per acre. Clearing just 2 % of the land (87,120 ft²) costs $435,600, or $4,356 per acre. Constructing a 16‑ft‑wide gravel lane costs $7.50 per linear foot[6]. A half‑mile (2,640 ft) lane therefore costs $19,800, or $198 per acre.
Per‑acre cost: Depending on how much land is cleared for infrastructure, site preparation ranges from $4,356 per acre (2 % clearing) to $21,780 per acre (10 % clearing). Lane construction adds $198 per acre.
3.3 Soil Amendments and Lime
After soil tests, farmers may need to apply lime to raise soil pH. Iowa State University budgets allocate $11.05 per acre per year for lime[7]. Fertility management costs appear later under annual maintenance. Minor soil amendments such as compost or gypsum vary widely and are not priced consistently.
Per‑acre cost: Plan to spend $11 per acre annually on lime[7] during establishment.
3.4 Subsurface Tile Drainage
Many Michigan soils are poorly drained. Iowa State University’s “Understanding the Economics of Tile Drainage” provides a simple example: installing tile drainage at $500 per acre increases annual net returns by $100 per acre, yielding a payback period of five years[8]. In practice, tile systems in the Midwest cost $800–$1,400 per acre depending on tile spacing and outlet construction. Properly installed drainage improves yields and reduces compaction.
Per‑acre cost: Installing tile drains costs $500–$1,400 per acre and requires occasional outlet maintenance. Using the $500 per acre example yields an annualized cost of $100 per acre (assuming a five‑year payback)[8].
4 Water Supply and Irrigation
4.1 Water Requirements and Permitting
Field crops in Michigan require approximately 7 acre‑inches of irrigation water per year[9], or about 20 cm. Irrigation systems must deliver 5–7 gallons per minute per irrigated acre to meet evapotranspiration demand[9]. Wells that withdraw more than 70 gallons per minute must be registered, and surface‑water withdrawals may require permits. The cost of drilling a well depends on depth and geology; no Michigan‑specific extension sources provide numbers, so well costs are not included in per‑acre budgets.
Per‑acre cost: Permitting and registration fees are modest (<$2 per acre) and are included in administrative overhead.

4.3 Irrigation Energy and Herbicide Activation
Delivering an acre‑inch of irrigation water costs roughly $7.19 per acre‑inch on average in Michigan[9]. Electric pumps cost $5.49 per acre‑inch and diesel pumps $8.90 per acre‑inch[9]. Applying the recommended 7 acre‑inches of water costs about $50 per acre (7 × $7.19). Irrigation also aids herbicide incorporation. An MSU article notes that poor rainfall may necessitate a second herbicide application costing $30–$40 per acre, plus $10–$12 for application[11]. Irrigating a half‑inch of water (costing $3.50 per acre) soon after herbicide application can activate herbicides and reduce the need for reapplication[11].
Per‑acre cost: Irrigation energy costs about $50 per acre for 7 acre‑inches[9]. Adding a half‑inch of water for herbicide activation costs $3.50 per acre, which may save $30–$52 in additional herbicide and application costs[11].
5 Fencing and Paddock Design
5.1 Perimeter and Paddock Fencing
MSU Extension’s Livestock Fencing Basics bulletin compares common fence types. A four‑rail board fence costs $9–$10 per foot; a five‑strand barbed‑wire fence costs $1.55–$2.25 per foot; woven wire costs $2.00–$4.50 per foot; high‑tensile electric (four‑wire) costs $0.95–$1.25 per foot; portable flex‑net electric costs $0.90–$1.15 per foot; and single‑strand poly wire costs $0.20 per foot[12]. Fence lifespan varies from 10 years for barbed wire to 20–30 years for high‑tensile electric[12]. Installing a four‑wire high‑tensile fence around a 40‑acre square paddock (perimeter ≈ 5,280 ft) costs $5,016–$6,600[12]. Per acre, this equates to $125–$165 for a 40‑acre paddock and about $58 per farm acre when averaged over 100 acres.
Per‑acre cost: High‑tensile perimeter fencing costs $58–$165 per acre, depending on paddock size. Temporary internal fences using poly wire cost $0.20 per foot, allowing cross‑fencing for under $5 per acre.
5.2 Fence Maintenance
High‑tensile fences last 20–30 years with minimal maintenance[12]. Annualized maintenance, including occasional post replacement and energizer costs, can be estimated by dividing installation cost by 25 years; for the example paddock, annual maintenance is about $5.81 per acre.
Per‑acre cost: Fence maintenance adds $6 per acre per year.
6 Buildings and Infrastructure
6.1 Machinery Sheds and Livestock Barns
Cornell Cooperative Extension’s building cost guide lists costs for common agricultural structures. Post‑frame (pole) buildings cost $8.40–$14.40 per square foot[6]. A 40 × 60 ft machinery shed (2,400 ft²) therefore costs $20,160–$34,560, or $202–$346 per acre when spread across 100 acres. Hoop structures (fabric‑covered frames) cost $7.25 per square foot[6], making a 2,400 ft² hoop shed $17,400 (or $174 per acre). Conventional stud‑wall buildings cost $18 per square foot[6], raising the cost of a 2,400 ft² utility building to $43,200 (or $432 per acre). Freestall dairy barns are more expensive at $22–$26 per square foot[6], equating to $48,400–$57,200 for a 10,000 ft² barn and $484–$572 per acre on a 100‑acre farm.
Per‑acre cost: Machinery storage ranges from $174–$432 per acre, depending on structure type. Adding a dairy barn increases infrastructure costs by an additional $484–$572 per acre.[6]
6.2 High Tunnels and Greenhouses
High tunnels (unheated hoop houses) extend the growing season for vegetables and small fruits. Kentucky State University’s high‑tunnel fact sheet states that high‑tunnel materials (excluding labor) cost $1.50–$5.00 per square foot[13], and a medium‑sized tunnel (30 × 96 ft or 2,880 ft²) costs $4,320–$14,400[13]. Drip irrigation for such tunnels costs $300–$1,000, and annual maintenance (replacing plastic and repairing irrigation) costs $100–$1,000[14]. High‑tunnel kits with metal hoops range from $2.25–$5.00 per square foot[15]. Oklahoma State University notes that high‑tunnel material costs can be as low as $0.50 per square foot, whereas greenhouses can cost up to $20 per square foot[16].
To compute a per‑acre cost, multiply cost per square foot by 43,560 ft². High tunnels at $1.50–$5.00 per ft² therefore cost $65,340–$217,800 per acre, while inexpensive hoop houses at $0.50 per ft² cost $21,780 per acre. In practice, farmers typically cover only a small fraction of an acre; for example, a 2,880‑ft² high tunnel costing $4,320–$14,400 equates to $43–$144 per acre (spread over 100 acres).
Per‑acre cost: A high tunnel covering 2,880 ft² costs $43–$144 per farm acre. Covering an entire acre with high tunnels would cost $21,780–$217,800 per acre depending on materials[13][15][16].
6.3 Grain and Feed Storage
On‑farm grain bins provide storage and marketing flexibility. Mississippi State University surveyed producers and found that constructing grain bins costs $4.00–$4.50 per bushel[17]. Example costs were $270,000 for a 60,000‑bushel bin, $340,000 for an 80,000‑bushel bin and $490,000 for a 120,000‑bushel bin[18]. When amortized over the life of the bin, costs equate to $0.42–$0.46 per bushel per year[18]. Ownership expenses (depreciation, taxes, insurance and repairs) range from 4–10 % of the establishment cost[19]. To store grain from a 100‑acre corn farm (170 bushels per acre, 17,000 bushels total), a 20,000‑bushel bin would suffice; using the $340,000 80,000‑bushel example as a guide (allowing for expansion), the cost per farm acre is about $3,400.
Grain storage bags are a flexible alternative. University of Tennessee Extension notes that bags hold 10,000–18,000 bushels and cost 6–17 cents per bushel[20]. Storing 20,000 bushels would require two bags costing $1,200–$3,400 (or $12–$34 per acre). Bag loaders and unloaders add additional capital costs; producers should place bags on well‑drained sites and monitor for tears[20].
Per‑acre cost: Constructing a grain bin capable of storing 17,000 bushels costs roughly $3,400 per acre when averaged over 100 acres. Using grain bags costs $12–$34 per acre but is temporary[17][20].
6.4 Poultry Housing (Optional Enterprise)
For producers considering broiler production, University of Maryland Extension provides a broiler enterprise budget for two tunnel‑ventilated houses measuring 60 × 550 ft (33,000 ft² total). The budget lists a tunnel house cost of $360,000 for two houses, equivalent to $5.45 per square foot[22]. Equipment costs $180,000 (or $2.73 per ft²) and site preparation $20,000 (or $0.30 per ft²)[23]. The total investment is $637,800[24]. If these houses occupy 6 acres, the investment amounts to roughly $106,300 per acre. Annual fixed costs (depreciation, interest, taxes and insurance) total $70,226, or $1.06 per ft²[25]; variable costs for electricity, repairs and cleaning add another $31,711 per flock[26]. Broiler production is often managed under contract with integrators and requires specialized equipment.
Per‑acre cost: Building two broiler houses requires a capital investment of $106,300 per acre and annual fixed costs of roughly $10,000 per acre, assuming six acres devoted to the facility[22][24]. This enterprise is optional and may not suit all farms.
7 Machinery and Equipment
7.1 Tractors and Self‑Propelled Machinery
MSU’s 2026 Custom Work Rates report lists purchase prices and ownership costs for tractors and combines. A 40 hp tractor costs $33,000[27] with ownership cost $6.13 per hour and fuel/lubrication $1.11 per hour[28]. A 75 hp tractor costs $65,000[29] and a 105 hp mid‑sized tractor costs $173,000 with ownership cost $16.10 per hour[29]. A 160 hp tractor costs $299,000 with ownership cost $24.53 per hour[29]. A 275 hp combine without a head costs $447,000 with ownership cost $42.16 per hour[29]. Farmers can purchase implements such as planters, drills and sprayers or hire custom operators. Custom rates include depreciation, fuel and labor and are discussed in the maintenance section.
Per‑acre cost: Purchasing a 105‑hp tractor and a 40‑hp utility tractor for a 100‑acre farm costs $206,000, or $2,060 per acre. Buying a combine adds $4,470 per acre. Renting or custom hiring equipment can reduce capital outlay.
7.2 Implements and Attachments
Implements such as plows, field cultivators, planters, drills and sprayers range widely in price depending on width and configuration. Because academic sources rarely list purchase prices, this report assumes farmers use custom hire. For example, the MSU custom work summary lists chisel plowing at $19.81 per acre[30], row‑crop planting at $19.85 per acre[30], boom spraying at $9.62 per acre[30] and cultivating at $10.34 per acre[30]. These costs include equipment, labor, fuel and repairs.
Per‑acre cost: Hiring custom operators for tillage, planting and spraying costs about $50 per acre (sum of typical operations), eliminating the need to purchase the implements. For farms that own implements, annual depreciation and repairs must be calculated separately.
8 Enterprise‑Specific Establishment Costs
8.1 Apple Orchards
MSU Extension’s Michigan Apple Cost of Production 2025 budget differentiates high‑density and semidwarf orchards. Planting a high‑density orchard costs $26,578 per acre and total establishment (land preparation, trees, trellis, irrigation and deer fence) costs $41,344 per acre, which is amortized to $2,067 per acre per year over 20 years[31]. A semidwarf orchard costs $7,676 per acre to plant and $21,250 per acre to establish, amortized to $1,062 per acre per year[31]. Pre‑plant costs include clearing, subsoiling, lime and seeding, and per‑acre irrigation equipment costs about $700 for a well and $400 for a trickle system[32]. Deer fencing adds $500 per acre[32].
Per‑acre cost: Establishing a high‑density apple orchard costs $41,344 per acre (amortized at $2,067 per year)[31]. A semidwarf orchard costs $21,250 per acre (amortized at $1,062 per year)[31].
8.2 Blueberries
The MSU Extension budget for blueberries indicates that establishing highbush blueberry fields cost $23,956 per acre, and total production costs in the first bearing year exceeded $10,000 per acre[33]. Establishment costs include land preparation, plants, drip irrigation, trellising, frost protection and labor during the non‑bearing years. Maintenance costs include pruning, fertilization, weed control, pest management and irrigation.
Per‑acre cost: Establishing a blueberry planting costs $23,956 per acre[33]. Annual production expenses for mature blueberries are approximately $10,000 per acre[33].
8.3 Peaches
The 2024 MSU peach cost of production budget estimates establishment costs of $9,167 per acre for processing peaches and $10,107 per acre for fresh peaches[34]. Annual production costs are $5,355 per acre for processing peaches and $7,587 per acre for fresh peaches[34]. These costs cover planting, training, pruning, fertilization, pest control, irrigation and harvesting.
Per‑acre cost: Establishing a peach orchard costs $9,167–$10,107 per acre[34]. Annual production costs range from $5,355–$7,587 per acre[34].
8.4 Cabbage and Vegetables
MSU Extension reports that producing fresh‑market cabbage costs nearly $7,700 per acre, whereas processing cabbage costs $4,140 per acre[35]. Harvesting accounts for more than half of the cost[35]. Costs for broccoli, carrots or lettuce would differ but are of similar magnitude.
Per‑acre cost: Fresh market cabbage costs $7,700 per acre, and processing cabbage costs $4,140 per acre[35].
8.5 Pasture Establishment
Iowa State University’s pasture improvement guide shows that renovating a pasture with a legume–grass mixture costs $287.85 per acre in initial expenses and that grass seeding costs $273.25 per acre[36]. A simpler grass improvement costs $105.55 per acre[36]. Annual maintenance and amortized establishment costs total $182.33 per acre for the legume–grass mixture, $175.25 per acre for grass seeding and $96.88 per acre for grass improvement[37]. Costs include machinery operations, fertilizer, herbicide, seed and labor[38].
Per‑acre cost: Establishing new pasture costs $105–$288 per acre, and annual maintenance ranges from $97–$182 per acre[36][37].

10 Conclusion
Building a commercial farm in Michigan requires significant capital investments beyond the cost of land. Site preparation, drainage, irrigation, fencing, buildings, machinery, grain storage, high tunnels and renewable energy systems contribute to start‑up costs that can range from $19,700 to $35,100 per acre depending on how much land is cleared for infrastructure. Enterprise‑specific investments, such as apple orchards ($21,250–$41,344 per acre), blueberry fields ($23,956 per acre) and peach orchards ($9,167–$10,107 per acre), can further increase capital requirements. Annual maintenance costs for row‑crop production with partial irrigation are around $948 per acre, while specialty crops like blueberries may cost $10,000 per acre each year. Thorough planning, careful budgeting, adherence to GAAMPs and diversification of enterprises are essential for the profitability and sustainability of Michigan farms.
